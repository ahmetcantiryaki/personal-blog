If you have 30 days for technical interview preparation, split the plan into four weeks: week 1 covers core data structures and patterns, week 2 tackles medium-hard algorithm problems, week 3 handles system design and behavioral questions, and week 4 is all mock interviews plus patching weak spots. The checklist below ties each day to concrete goals; 2-3 hours a day is enough.

This isn't a fantasy schedule. It mirrors the rhythm developers actually follow when they change jobs three times in two years and pass FAANG and scaling-startup loops. The goal isn't solving 500 problems — it's recalling the right patterns under pressure.

## How do you prepare for a technical interview in 30 days?

Thirty days of technical interview preparation isn't enough to go from zero to expert, but it's plenty to fit what you already know into interview format. At 2-3 focused hours a day, you get 60-75 hours total. Spend that time on pattern recognition and thinking out loud, not rote problem grinding; that's what actually decides the interview.

Most candidates misallocate their time: they grind 300 LeetCode problems but never do a single mock. The result is a candidate who knows the material yet freezes under pressure. This plan flips the balance — the final week is dedicated entirely to verbal rehearsal.

### Before you start: an honest self-assessment

Spend day one measuring, not studying. Solve two medium problems on a timer and answer these three questions:

- When you see a problem, do you spot **which pattern** it is within the first 5 minutes?
- Do you stall when explaining your solution **out loud**?
- Can you state the Big-O analysis **without thinking**?

Those three answers decide which week of the plan below you lean into hardest.

## The 30-day technical interview prep checklist

The program below breaks into four weeks. Each week builds on the last, so don't skip the order.

1. **Days 1-2: Measure and set up.** Do the self-assessment, pin down target companies and role level, and prepare your workspace (IDE, timer, notebook).
2. **Days 3-7: Data structure foundation.** Review arrays, hash maps, linked lists, stacks/queues, trees, and heaps one topic per day, and solve 3-4 easy problems per topic.
3. **Days 8-14: Pattern-driven algorithms.** Learn two pointers, sliding window, BFS/DFS, backtracking, and dynamic programming; do 4-5 medium problems per pattern.
4. **Days 15-19: System design basics.** Study scaling, caching, database choice, queues, and API design; rehearse walking through one design end to end in 45 minutes.
5. **Days 20-23: Behavioral prep.** Write 8-10 stories in STAR format covering leadership, conflict, failure, and impact themes.
6. **Days 24-28: Mock interviews.** Do one full mock a day (coding plus design or behavioral), ideally with another person; record and rewatch each session.
7. **Day 29: Weak-spot repair.** Go back to the 2-3 topics you stalled on most in the mocks; don't open new topics.
8. **Day 30: Light review and rest.** Skim your notes, rebuild confidence with a few easy problems, and sleep early. Burning yourself out the day before never helps.

## What should you focus on week by week?

In technical interview preparation, each week needs one clear objective; studying everything at once scatters you. The table below sums up the weekly focus, daily time, and the output you should reach by the end of that week.

| Week | Main focus | Daily time | End output |
|------|-----------|------------|------------|
| 1 | Data structures + easy problems | 2 hrs | Build any structure from memory |
| 2 | Algorithm patterns (medium) | 2.5 hrs | Recognize a pattern in 5 minutes |
| 3 | System design + behavioral | 2.5 hrs | Walk a design in 45 minutes |
| 4 | Mock interviews | 3 hrs | Think fluently under pressure |

Notice the difficulty climbs linearly but the time doesn't. The final week runs longer because mocks include a feedback-and-correction loop. Keeping week 1 light is deliberate; it prevents early burnout.

## How should you practice algorithms?

The most common mistake is staring at a problem you can't solve for hours. Set a 25-minute timer; when it runs out, read the solution, understand it, and **write the pattern in your notebook**. The next day, re-solve the same problem with a blank sheet. The goal is active recall, not solution count.

Run this loop for every problem:

```text
1. Restate the problem in your own words (2 min)
2. Find edge cases with sample input-output (3 min)
3. Explain the approach OUT LOUD, no code yet (5 min)
4. State the Big-O, then code it (10 min)
5. Verify with your own tests (5 min)
```

This loop is a direct rehearsal of the interview. Don't skip step 3; in a real interview, the evaluator watches your thinking far more than your code. Writing correct code in silence is a low-scoring performance.

## How do you prepare for a system design interview?

System design is the most neglected but level-defining part of technical interview preparation. Even junior roles may ask basic scaling; at mid and senior it's decisive. You should be able to take a 45-minute design from requirements through the data model, API, and bottlenecks.

Follow this skeleton in your rehearsals:

- **Clarify requirements:** functional and non-functional (scale, latency, consistency).
- **Do the back-of-envelope math:** daily active users, requests/second, storage estimate.
- **Draw the high-level architecture:** client, API layer, services, database, cache.
- **Go deep:** pick one component and discuss the data model and trade-offs.
- **Address bottlenecks:** single points of failure, scaling, cache invalidation.

The most common mistake is diving into the diagram before asking about requirements. Strong candidates spend the first 5 minutes asking questions. For backend depth, our [Docker best practices for production](/en/docker-best-practices-production) gives practical infrastructure context.

## Don't underestimate behavioral questions

Even technically flawless candidates get cut in the behavioral round. Preparation means pre-written STAR (Situation, Task, Action, Result) stories. Write 8-10 stories and keep each flexible enough to adapt to more than one question.

Themes you need to cover:

- How you resolved a conflict or disagreement
- A project where you failed and what you learned from it
- Work where you created measurable impact (with numbers)
- A moment you took initiative and owned something

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is 30 days enough for technical interview preparation?

Yes, if you have a foundation. Thirty days isn't designed to learn from scratch — it fits your existing knowledge into interview format. At 2-3 hours a day you get 60-75 hours; spend it on pattern recognition and mocks and you'll be ready for most loops. If you've never written code, 30 days won't cut it.

### How many problems should I solve per day?

Quality beats quantity. Aim for 2-4 problems a day, but solve each deeply with the five-step loop above. Solving three problems while internalizing their patterns is worth more than skimming thirty. Once you hit medium difficulty in week two, the pace naturally slows.

### Should I study algorithms or system design first?

Algorithms come first because most loops open with a coding round and it builds the foundation. This plan gives the first two weeks to algorithms and the third to system design. If you're targeting a senior or staff role, weight system design more heavily; at that level it's the deciding factor.

### Do I really need mock interviews?

Yes — they're the most critical part of the plan. Thinking out loud under pressure is a completely different skill from solving alone, and it only improves with rehearsal. Do at least 3-4 mocks a week with a friend, mentor, or online platform; recording and rewatching each session surfaces weak spots fast.
