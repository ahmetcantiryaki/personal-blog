If you're deciding whether to buy AI smart glasses this year or wait, here's the short version: Meta already shipped a display model and controller band, Google and Samsung's answer lands as audio-only glasses this fall with a display version later, and the whole category is projected to roughly triple in unit sales in 2026. Which one is worth it depends entirely on whether you need an in-lens display today or can wait for Gemini's ecosystem to catch up.

## The Market Is Actually Taking Off This Time

Smart glasses have failed to break through before, but the numbers for 2026 look different. Industry estimates put global AI smart-glasses sales volume rising from roughly 6 million units in 2025 to about 20 million units in 2026, with market value expanding from an estimated $1.2 billion to $5.6 billion over the same stretch, according to [The Gadgeteer's 2026 roundup](https://the-gadgeteer.com/2026/05/25/smart-glasses-2026-design-strategy/). Meta's own disclosures back up the momentum from its side alone: the company says it sold over 7 million Ray-Ban Meta glasses in 2025, roughly tripling its prior year's volume. Two different sources, two different scopes — but both point the same direction: this is the first year smart glasses look like a real consumer category instead of a demo.

## Meta Ray-Ban Display: The Expensive, Available-Now Option

Meta's flagship device is the Ray-Ban Display, priced at $799, and it's built around two things competitors don't have yet: an actual in-lens display and the Neural Band, a wristband that reads surface electromyography (sEMG) signals from your forearm muscles. That band lets you pinch, swipe, and scroll without the camera needing to see your hand — useful in low light, in a pocket, or when you'd rather not wave your arm around in public. Meta also opened the Ray-Ban Display platform to developers, according to [Android Central's coverage](https://www.androidcentral.com/gaming/virtual-reality/metas-ray-ban-display-build-for-the-future-opens-its-doors-to-developers), signaling it wants third-party apps rather than a closed Meta-only feature set.

Below the Display sits Meta's cheaper Adventurer/Fury line at $299, which drops the in-lens screen entirely and behaves more like the original audio-first Ray-Ban Meta glasses: camera, speakers, mic, and Meta AI, no visual overlay.

## Android XR: Audio First, Display Later

Google and Samsung previewed their answer at I/O 2026, and the strategy is different: ship the easier, cheaper form factor first. [Android Central's analysis](https://www.androidcentral.com/wearables/why-ray-ban-meta-glasses-fans-should-be-excited-by-google-samsung-android-xr-glasses) confirms Samsung told investors on its Q4 2025 earnings call that Android XR audio glasses — built with partners including Warby Parker and Gentle Monster — are launching this fall, with display-equipped Android XR glasses following afterward. Neither the audio nor the display model has a confirmed name or price yet, which is the single biggest reason to consider waiting.

What Android XR does have is Gemini baked in from day one as the assistant layer, plus Google's existing strengths: live translation, turn-by-turn navigation tied to Google Maps, and direct access to whatever Gemini can already do across Search, Photos, and Workspace. Meta's glasses use Meta AI instead, which is capable but doesn't have the same native reach into a search and productivity ecosystem.

```text
Buy Ray-Ban Display now if: you want an in-lens display today and

(Maps, Photos, Workspace) and value translation and navigation over
```

## What This Means If You're Building for Either Platform

For developers, Meta's decision to open the Ray-Ban Display platform now is the more actionable signal — there's an actual SDK and app surface to target today, versus Android XR's developer story, which is still largely promises tied to a fall launch. If your product idea depends on Gemini's ecosystem specifically (Workspace integration, Maps-based navigation, multimodal search), it's worth prototyping against the Gemini API on a phone now so you're ready to port once Android XR glasses actually ship, rather than waiting for hardware that has no confirmed date.

My take: the interesting fight here isn't display resolution or field of view — it's Neural Band-style gesture input versus voice-and-ecosystem depth. Meta bet that a genuinely new input method is worth $799 and an unfamiliar app store. Google bet that shipping the cheaper, simpler form factor first and layering Gemini's existing strengths on top will win more buyers than a flashy display would. Given how thin the margin for daily wearability is on any face-worn device, I'd bet on the ecosystem play outlasting the flashier hardware — but Meta's year-plus head start on real developer platform maturity is not nothing.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is Meta's Ray-Ban Display worth $799 right now?

If you specifically want an in-lens display and gesture input via the Neural Band today, yes — it's the only device on the market with both. If you can live without a display, the $299 Adventurer/Fury line gets most of the AI-glasses experience for much less.

### When do Android XR smart glasses actually launch?

Samsung has confirmed audio-only Android XR glasses, built with partners like Warby Parker and Gentle Monster, are launching in fall 2026. A display-equipped Android XR model is planned to follow, but neither has a confirmed name or price yet.

### Does Meta or Google have more market share in smart glasses?

Meta reports selling over 7 million Ray-Ban Meta glasses in 2025, roughly tripling the prior year, giving it a substantial head start. Android XR devices haven't shipped in volume yet, so a direct share comparison isn't possible until the fall 2026 launch data comes in.

### Should I wait for a smart-glasses model with both a display and Gemini?

If that specific combination matters to you, yes — it doesn't exist yet. Google's roadmap points to a Gemini-powered display model eventually, but Samsung's own comments suggest the audio-first version ships first, with the display version coming later and undated.
